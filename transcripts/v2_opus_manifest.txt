The key subtlety here is a decoding trap: the `survived` column is actually a **loss-of-life claim flag** — `1` means the passenger *died* (a claim was filed), not survived. Two pieces of documentation confirm this:

- The data dictionary defines `survived = 1` as "loss-of-life claim filed."
- The loss abstract states 530 were lost and 779 recovered — and in the data, exactly 530 rows have `survived==1` and 779 have `survived==0`.

So true survivors are the `survived==0` rows. After correcting for that:

**1. Overall survival rate:** 779 of 1,309 recovered = **59.51%** (matches the archive's "779 recovered").

**2. Highest-survival group:** **male, 3rd class** — 415 of 493 = **84.18%**.

For the exhibit, one note worth flagging to curators: because the ledger records *claims*, the survival pattern runs opposite to the familiar "women and children first" narrative (1st-class women survived at only ~2%). This reflects how the insurance ledger was structured, not necessarily lifeboat priority — the museum may want to caveat what the source actually measures.
