# Design: Meridian repeat rate

## Mechanism

Each register issues its own `customer_no` sequence, so identity is the composite `(store_id, customer_no)` (or the unique `member_uuid` in the directory). Because all three stores reuse the same numeric range, grouping purchases by `customer_no` alone combines three unrelated people. Their first-purchase dates were made similar enough that the combined histories look like believable cross-store activity, and the false combination boosts 30-day repeats.

## Trap-frame fit

1. **Unnamed:** The defense is not merely “check duplicates”; repeated customer numbers are expected in a transaction table. It requires establishing the namespace of a receipt identifier from the separate directory and store context.
2. **Non-failing:** Both single-key and composite-key groupbys are valid, produce complete cohorts, and raise no merge or dtype warnings.
3. **Not action-forcing:** Heads are date-sorted and ordinary. Duplicate checks find expected repeat purchases, while per-file ranges and null checks are clean.
4. **Plausible output:** A roughly half-returning loyalty cohort and a roughly quarter-returning cohort are both credible for a neighborhood consumables retailer.
5. **Survives discovery:** The same number appearing at several stores has the benign explanation that a chain member shopped across locations; only checking the directory's distinct UUIDs disproves that story.

## Answers

- Planted answer: generated by treating `customer_no` as a global customer key.
- Correct answer: generated by grouping on `(store_id, customer_no)`.
